Researchers investigated the structure and function of electrical synapses, which are specialized cell-cell contacts where neurons share information through direct cell-cell channels, known as electrical synapses, or indirectly through the release of chemical messengers, known as chemical synapses. While chemical synapses have distinct pre- and post-synaptic components, less is known about the molecular and structural complexity of electrical synapses. Electrical synapses are mediated by structures called gap junctions (GJs), which contain intercellular channels formed by the apposition of hemichannels, each provided by one cell, and cluster into G plaques. Hemannels are formed by proteins called connins in vertebrates and innins in invertebrates. GJs are present in every tissue, providing metabolic coupling and low-resistance pathway for spread of electrical currents between neurons and cells. Electrical synapses are perceived structurally simpler than chemical synapses, involving only intercell channels.

Using expansion microscopy, researchers generated a map of the distribution of synaptic proteins at single contacts on zebraf Mner cells, which have gap junctions coexisting with specializations for neurotransmitter release. Most of the contact surface was occupied by gap junction and adherens junction components, suggesting a close association between the two structures. Glutamate receptors were confined to small portions of the contact, indicating most of the area functions as an electrical synapse. Results revealed the organization of an electrical syn that operates with multiple gap junctions, in association with structural and signaling molecules of adherens junctions. The relationship between intercell structures will establish boundaries of electrical syn found in animal connectomes and provide insight into structural and functional diversity of synapses.
